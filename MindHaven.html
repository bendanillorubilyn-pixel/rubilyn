The Manifesto

I built this Mind Haven to share comfort and peace. Having gone through my own challenges, I wanted to create a space where everyone can find rest, relief, and healing for their mind and soul.

Mental Experience

Ever since in high school, I felt like I was living in total darkness. I was trapped alone in an empty house while my parents were working, my sisters were far away studying, and silence was the only thing keeping me company. Every night felt endless, and the loneliness slowly ate me alive until anxiety and depression took full control.

When the pandemic came, my mind became a living nightmare. I was drowning in fear and pain, but I couldn't scream for help because I was terrified of being judged. I kept every terrifying thought locked inside my head, and the more I stayed silent, the louder the chaos became. It felt like I was being consumed by something I couldn't see or fight.

By Grade 9, I completely lost myself. I stopped caring about my studies and buried myself in my phone just to escape reality. When the first quarter results came and my name wasn't on the honor list, I tried to convince myself it was nothing. I made it back on the list for the second quarter, but the darkness pulled me back down again. Every time I tried to focus, panic would attack me like a wild beast, crushing me with pressure until I couldn't breathe. I chose to run away instead of facing the pain.

Then came the day that changed everything. Our teacher posted the list of students who would graduate with honors. I stared at the screen, trembling, searching for my name... but it wasn't there. My heart stopped. I messaged my teacher, begging it was just a mistake, but her reply crushed me: "89."

Fear paralyzed me. I wasn't scared for myself, but I was terrified of my parents. I had always been the honor student, and I knew their expectations were high. I couldn't face them. I couldn't handle the disappointment. The weight was too heavy to carry.

In that moment of total despair, the voices in my head screamed louder than ever. I felt like death was the only way out. I walked towards the cabinet with shaking hands, my heart pounding like a drum. I opened the bottles of medicine, one by one, and poured them into my palm. It looked like a handful of white death.

Without hesitation, I swallowed them all. Pill after pill after pill. I could feel my throat burning, my vision starting to blur, and my body getting heavy. I laid down, waiting for the end. The room started to spin, the silence became deafening, and slowly, everything turned pitch black. I could feel my soul slipping away, leaving my body behind. I was ready to never wake up again.

When I opened my eyes again, the bright white lights of the hospital stabbed my eyes. I was alive, but I felt empty inside. They asked me why I did it, but I couldn't speak—I was still trapped in fear, still seeing the darkness I came back from. The next day, I finally found the courage to tell them the truth. I told them about the anxiety, the pressure, and the terror I felt inside.

Action Protocol

Healing was not an overnight journey, but I slowly learned how to take back control of my life. I realized that I couldn't keep running away from my feelings, so I started facing them gently.

I learned to validate myself. Instead of telling myself that my feelings were wrong or that I was weak, I started saying, "It is okay to feel this way, but I am stronger than this." I stopped seeking validation from others and started listening to what my mind and heart truly needed.

I also discovered that distraction is different from healing. While using my phone helped me forget for a while, true peace came when I learned to sit with my emotions, breathe, and understand where they were coming from. I started accepting that my worth is not defined by my grades or by what others expect of me.

What I Do When It Attacks

1. I Stop and Breathe

When my mind starts to race and I feel overwhelmed, I pause everything. I take deep, slow breaths. I remind myself: "This feeling is temporary. It will pass, just like all the other times."

2. I Talk to Myself Softly

Instead of scolding myself or panicking, I speak gently. I say, "You are safe. You are not alone. You have survived this before, and you will survive it now."

3. I Write It Down

I pour all the chaos in my head onto paper or into my notes. Seeing my thoughts written down makes them feel smaller and more manageable. It helps me realize that they are just thoughts, not facts.

4. I Do Something I Love

I shift my focus to things that bring me peace—whether it’s listening to calming music, watching something funny, or simply resting. I give myself permission to pause and take care of myself.

5. I Remember My Why

I remember that I survived the darkest days, and I created this space to help others. That purpose gives me strength to keep going and not let the darkness win.
